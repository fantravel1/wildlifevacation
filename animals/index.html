Expert Answers

Wildlife Viewing FAQs

Common questions answered by our team of wildlife biologists and guides

What is the best time of day to see wildlife on safari? +

The best times for wildlife viewing are early morning (6-9am) and late afternoon (4-6:30pm). These are cooler periods when predators are most activeâ€”lions often hunt at dawn, and leopards become active at dusk.

Many animals rest during the midday heat, making game viewing between 11am-3pm less productive. However, waterholes can be excellent midday locations as animals come to drink.

Night drives (where permitted) offer opportunities to see nocturnal species like leopards hunting, aardvarks, bush babies, civets, and African wild cats.

How close can you get to wild animals? +

Ethical viewing distances vary by species, context, and local regulations:

  • Safari vehicles: Animals often approach within 10-20 meters as they're habituated to vehicles. Never exit the vehicle near dangerous animals.
  • Gorilla trekking: A minimum 7-meter (21 feet) distance is maintained to protect gorillas from human diseases.
  • Whale watching: Boats must keep 100+ meters; whales often approach closer out of curiosity.
  • Snorkeling with marine life: Maintain 3+ meters from sea turtles, 10+ meters from whales and whale sharks.
  • Walking safaris: Typically observe from further distances; your armed guide determines safe approach distances.

Your guide will ensure safe, legal, and ethical distances that minimize animal stress. Never chase, corner, or pressure wildlife for a photo.

Are wildlife encounters safe for children? +

Most wildlife viewing is very safe for children when following guide instructions. Safari vehicles provide excellent protection, and children are often more observant than adults at spotting animals!

Age restrictions to note:

  • Game drives: Most lodges accept children 6+; some family lodges have no minimum
  • Walking safaris: Usually 12-16 minimum age
  • Gorilla trekking: 15 years minimum (Rwanda/Uganda law)
  • Scuba diving: 10-12 minimum for junior certification

Family-friendly destinations: Costa Rica, GalÃ¡pagos, South Africa (malaria-free areas), and Australia offer exceptional wildlife experiences suitable for all ages with excellent infrastructure.

What animals can you see on an African safari? +

African safaris offer incredible biodiversity. Commonly seen species (almost guaranteed on a 3+ day safari):

  • Elephants, giraffes, zebras, wildebeest, hippos, crocodiles
  • Numerous antelope species (impala, kudu, waterbuck, etc.)
  • Warthogs, baboons, vervet monkeys

Regularly seen (most safaris):

  • Lions, buffalo, spotted hyenas

Requires patience/luck:

  • Leopards, cheetahs, rhinos, wild dogs, servals

Bird diversity is exceptional with 500+ species in top destinations. The specific mix depends on habitat, season, and your destination.

How does wildlife tourism help conservation? +

Responsible wildlife tourism is one of the most powerful conservation tools available:

  • Economic value: A single lion is worth $500,000+ in tourism revenue over its lifetime vs. ~$1,000 to a trophy hunter or poacher. This makes live animals economically valuable to protect.
  • Habitat protection: Tourism revenue funds park management, anti-poaching patrols, and habitat preservation. Many reserves would close without tourist income.
  • Community benefit: When local communities benefit from tourism, they become wildlife protectors rather than poachers. Jobs, schools, and healthcare funded by tourism create conservation allies.
  • Direct funding: Gorilla permits ($1,500 in Rwanda) fund ranger salaries, veterinary care, and community programs. Tourism literally saved mountain gorillas from extinction.

Choose operators with clear conservation contributions, community partnerships, and sustainable practices to maximize your positive impact.

What photography equipment do I need for wildlife? +

Essential equipment:

  • Camera: Any modern DSLR or mirrorless camera with good autofocus. A high-quality smartphone can capture excellent memories too.
  • Lens: A 100-400mm or 200-600mm zoom is ideal for safaris. 70-200mm works for closer encounters. A 24-70mm captures landscapes and camp life.
  • Memory cards: Bring more than you think you'll needâ€”you'll shoot thousands of images.
  • Spare batteries: Cold weather and heavy use drain batteries quickly.
  • Beanbag: Essential for stabilizing long lenses on safari vehicles (better than tripods).

Nice to have: Teleconverters, rain covers, sensor cleaning kit, laptop for backup.

Pro tip: Many safari vehicles and boats have limited space. One camera body and one versatile zoom lens often works better than a heavy bag of equipment.
