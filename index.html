Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about planning your ethical wildlife adventure

The best time for an African safari depends on your destination and what you want to see:

  • East Africa (Kenya, Tanzania): June to October is dry season with excellent wildlife viewing. The Great Migration peaks July-September in the Serengeti/Mara.
  • Southern Africa (South Africa, Botswana, Zimbabwe): May to October offers optimal conditionsâ€”cooler temperatures, thinner vegetation, and animals concentrated around water.
  • Green season (November-April): Fewer crowds, lower prices, and excellent for birding and baby animals. Some areas may be inaccessible due to rain.

Yes! Many wildlife destinations are excellent for families with proper planning:

  • Best family destinations: South Africa's malaria-free reserves (Madikwe, Eastern Cape), Costa Rica, GalÃ¡pagos Islands, and Yellowstone.
  • Age recommendations: Most lodges welcome children 6+ on game drives. Some offer kids' programs from age 4.
  • Family-friendly features: Look for lodges with family rooms, shorter game drives, educational programs, and swimming pools.
  • Gorilla trekking: Minimum age is 15 years in Rwanda and Uganda.

We include family suitability ratings and age recommendations in all our destination guides.

Look for these indicators of ethical practices:

  • Certifications: Rainforest Alliance, Travelife, Global Sustainable Tourism Council, or local ecotourism bodies.
  • Conservation contributions: Direct funding to conservation projects, anti-poaching units, or research.
  • Community benefit: Employment of local guides, support for community projects, fair wages.
  • Wildlife welfare: No direct contact with wild animals, appropriate viewing distances, limited group sizes.

Red flags to avoid: Elephant riding, "walking with predators," captive animal experiences marketed as sanctuaries, guaranteed sightings.

Safari costs vary widely based on destination, accommodation level, and inclusions:

  • Budget safaris: $150-250/person/day â€” Basic camps, group game drives, self-drive options
  • Mid-range: $350-600/person/day â€” Comfortable lodges, private game drives, good guides
  • Luxury: $800-1,500+/person/day â€” Exclusive camps, top guides, all-inclusive experiences

Additional costs to budget:

  • Gorilla permits: $1,500 (Rwanda), $700 (Uganda)
  • Park fees: $50-100/day in most African parks
  • Internal flights: $150-500 between destinations
  • Gratuities: $10-25/day for guides and staff

The Big Five refers to Africa's most iconic safari animals: lion, leopard, elephant, rhinoceros, and Cape buffalo. Originally a hunting term for the most dangerous animals to pursue on foot.

Best destinations for all Big Five:

  • South Africa: Kruger National Park, Sabi Sands (best for leopards)
  • Kenya: Masai Mara, Ol Pejeta Conservancy (rhino sanctuary)
  • Tanzania: Serengeti, Ngorongoro Crater (rhinos in the crater)
  • Botswana: Okavango Delta, Chobe (massive elephant herds)

Rhino note: Due to poaching, rhinos are increasingly rare. Ngorongoro Crater and dedicated rhino conservancies offer the best chances.

Vaccination requirements depend on your destination. Consult a travel medicine specialist 6-8 weeks before departure.

Common requirements and recommendations:

  • Yellow fever: Required for many African and South American countries. Certificate may be checked at borders.
  • Recommended vaccines: Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid, Tetanus/Diphtheria, and sometimes Rabies (for remote areas)
  • Malaria prophylaxis: Recommended for most sub-Saharan African destinations. Some South African reserves are malaria-free.
  • COVID-19: Requirements vary; check current destination guidelines.

For wildlife photography, preparation makes a big difference:

Recommended gear:

  • Camera: DSLR or mirrorless with good autofocus and burst capability
  • Lenses: Telephoto (100-400mm or 200-600mm) essential; also bring a wide zoom (24-70mm) for landscapes and vehicle scenes
  • Support: Beanbag for vehicle stabilization (tripods rarely practical in game vehicles)
  • Extras: Multiple batteries (cold and heat drain them fast), plenty of memory cards, lens cleaning kit for dust

Smartphone note: Modern smartphones work well for scenic shots and social media, but struggle with distant wildlife. They're great for video clips!
